# AI coding contest day 14: HappyHexaminos. Claude packs all 35.

The fourteenth challenge is a hexomino packing puzzle. Each round the server sends the bot a rectangle of width `w` and height `h` with `w·h ≡ 0 (mod 6)` and area between 648 and 900 cells, and the bot must respond with a *full* tiling of the rectangle: every cell covered, no overlaps, every region a connected 6-cell shape. The score for the round is the bot's **inventory**, the count of *distinct* free hexominoes used. **Higher inventory wins**: pack as many of the 35 free hexominoes as possible into the rectangle while still completing a valid tiling. Identical shapes in different rotations or reflections count as one.

The format is a 10-round solo tournament. Areas grow monotonically across rounds: R1 is 24×27 (648 cells, 108 pieces), R10 is 30×30 (900 cells, 150 pieces). Each round, every bot has a 30-second wall-clock budget to send back a tiling. Per-round scoring runs top-3: 3 pts for the round's highest inventory (1st place), 2 pts for the next (2nd), 1 pt for the third (3rd). Ties on inventory break by earliest submission timestamp. Invalid or timed-out submissions score zero. Tournament total = sum of round points.

10 bots were generated by sending the prompt to each model via the OpenRouter API in a single one-shot call. Two bots dropped before the tournament: **MiMo** (V2.5-Pro) hit infinite chain-of-thought during generation across all three permitted attempts and never produced code, and **GLM** (5.1) returned 1019 lines containing a `nonlocal pid` declaration that comes after the variable is used. That's a SyntaxError, and the bot won't import. Both are DNF.

## Claude: every board, every shape

Claude (Opus 4.7, 456 lines) submits inventory **35** on every round: it produces a valid tiling that uses every one of the 35 free hexominoes at least once on each of the 10 boards. Its submission times cluster between 25.51 s and 26.72 s, sitting on roughly 88% of the 30-second budget every round.

The implementation is a focused exact-cover backtracker. The bot precomputes the 8 orientations of every hexomino once at startup, then for each round runs `_solve_inventory(w, h, deadline)`: depth-first search at the lowest-empty cell, trying placements in randomized order, with a `_has_dead_holes` pruner that rejects any placement leaving an empty region whose cell count isn't divisible by 6. The objective is to use **every** shape at least once before falling back to repeats. The search prioritizes orientations of unused shapes ahead of orientations already in inventory. A `_baseline_tile` deterministic 1×6/2×3 fallback is held in reserve for any round where the search runs out of clock, but that fallback never fires in the tournament: the optimization completes within budget on all 10 boards.

That's a real result. With 150 pieces on the 30×30 board, the solver must place 35 distinct shapes (210 cells) plus 115 cells of repeats (≈ 19 more pieces of any flavor), and every placement has to leave the remaining empty region tileable. The dead-hole pruner is what makes that tractable in 30 s.

## ChatGPT: 34 by construction, in 4.4 seconds

ChatGPT (GPT 5.5, 425 lines) submits inventory **34** every round, in **4.40 seconds** every round. The same wall-clock time, to two decimals, on all 10 boards. The cause is structural: the solver uses a fixed pre-solved block. `solve_exact_block` finds an exact-cover tiling of a small reference rectangle that uses 34 distinct shapes; the bot then places that block in one corner and fills the remaining L-shaped region with deterministic 2×3 / 1×6 brickwork via `fill_baseline`. The block is computed once per round; the placement and fill are O(area). Time-to-submit is dominated by the block solve, which is bounded by the small reference size and runs in roughly 4 s every round.

The cost of the construction approach is that the block size is fixed at 34 shapes, one short of Claude's 35, and the fill is single-shape. So ChatGPT is locked at 34 forever. No round-to-round variation, no chance of catching Claude. But it never fails either, and it banks 10 second-place finishes for 20 points uncontested.

## Gemini: DLX, run out of clock on the last two rounds

Gemini (Pro 3.1, 221 lines) implements **Algorithm X with Dancing Links**: a full DLX exact-cover solver over all placements of all 35 hexominoes, with a 28 s search budget per round. When DLX completes within budget, Gemini returns whatever cover the search finds first, which typically uses 30–34 distinct shapes (whichever happened to land in that cover).

Gemini scores 32, 33, 30, 33, 34, 33, 33, 30 on R1–R8, competitive with ChatGPT in two rounds (R5 specifically: 34 vs 34, lost the 2nd-place tiebreak by 14 s on submission timestamp), 3rd-place finishes in the others. On R9 (29×30, 145 pieces) and R10 (30×30, 150 pieces) the DLX search exceeds the 30 s deadline and Gemini sends nothing parseable: INV both rounds, 0 points. The implementation is correct; the boards just got too big for the bot's solver to finish on.

## Kimi: variable, but always submits

Kimi (K2.6, 357 lines) is the messiest implementation in the top 4. The bot has four separate strategies (`solve_block`, `simple_tiling_2x3`, `simple_tiling_1x6`, `mixed_tiling`, `greedy_diverse_tiling`) and dispatches based on `partition_wh`. The output is highly variable: 12 in R1, 15 in R2, jumps to 31 in R3, then back down to 18–24 for R4–R10.

The R3 spike (31 distinct hexominoes on a 24×29 board) is the bot's diverse-packing strategy succeeding on a partition where the others fail; the lower scores are the simple-tiling fallbacks taking over. Kimi never INVs and never times out (every submission is valid), but the diverse path only finds a high-inventory tiling on one round in ten. Three 3rd-place finishes (R3, R9, R10), 3 points.

## Bottom tier: greedy fallback or first-round disconnect

**Grok (Expert 4.20, 216 lines)** has the right idea: a greedy max-inventory placement followed by a guaranteed-tile fallback. Every round the greedy-pack phase fails to complete (the early shape choices block the remaining board) and the bot falls through to the inventory-1 fallback, which is just a single 1×6 or 2×3 tiling. So Grok submits inventory **1** every round, valid but minimum. 0 points.

**Nemotron (3 Super, 113 lines)** parses `ROUND <n> <w> <h>` with `if len(parts) != 3: continue`, but the line has 4 tokens, not 3. The bot skips every ROUND it receives and submits nothing. Server times out the round after 30 s. INV ×10.

**Muse (Spark, 144 lines)** ships with three pre-baked 6×6 hexomino templates as Python literals (`T_A`, `T_B`, `T_C`). The first template, `T_A`, has rows of length `[4, 4, 6, 6, 6, 6]`. The first two rows are only 4 cells wide instead of 6. `place_template` reads `tmpl[dy][dx]` with `dx in range(6)`, so any access to row 0 or row 1 with `dx >= 4` raises `IndexError`. The bot crashes on the first round and never recovers. INV ×10.

**DeepSeek (V4-Pro, 247 lines)** has the most ambitious solver in the bottom tier: a full DLX implementation with shape-set targeting (`required_shapes` parameter) and a 28-second search budget. The bot runs the search on R1, fails to find a valid solution within the budget, then disconnects (its connection state isn't recovered between rounds and it never re-engages). INV ×10.

## On bot generation

The two DNFs are different failure modes. MiMo's three regeneration attempts each terminated with `finish_reason=length` and 65535 reasoning tokens. The model burns its full thinking budget without emitting a single output token. By the rule, three ICoTL attempts is the cutoff: MiMo is DNF without ever producing a bot file. GLM, in contrast, produced 1019 lines of code with a syntax error that the model did not catch (`nonlocal pid` declared after `pid` is referenced). The Python interpreter rejects the file on import; the bot never starts.

The two failures bracket a useful distinction: MiMo is a model that can't think *and* answer in one budget on a hard problem, GLM is a model that finishes its answer but doesn't self-check it.

## The verdict

The metric matters. With "highest inventory wins" as the rule, the field separates into a clear ordinal: **Claude > ChatGPT > Gemini > Kimi > everyone else**. Claude is the only model that wrote a solver capable of fitting all 35 distinct hexominoes into every board within budget. ChatGPT wrote a solver that hits 34 instantly and stops looking. Gemini and Kimi wrote correct solvers that hit a wall on the larger boards or relied on simpler fallbacks. The bottom four broke down differently. Nemotron parsed `ROUND` with the wrong arity and never submitted, Muse shipped a malformed template that crashed on the first board, Grok's fallback ran every round, and DeepSeek's DLX never returned in time.

Across 10 rounds and 1248 total piece placements per top-tier bot (108 + 112 + ... + 150), Claude's 0 dropped rounds and 0 timeouts is the day's result.
