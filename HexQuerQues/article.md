# AI coding contest day 13: HexQuerQues. Kimi wins by one piece.

The thirteenth challenge: a two-player capture game on a board of four concentric hexagons connected by radial spokes. 24 vertices total, 6 per ring. Each player starts with 6 pieces in an alternating pattern across the two outermost rings. Movement is one step along a board line; capture is by jumping an adjacent enemy along a line to the empty vertex beyond. Captures are forced and chained. Win by capturing all 6 enemies, or by stalemating the opponent (no legal move). Classic Alquerques rules carried verbatim onto the hex topology.

The format is a **round-robin of 1v1 matchups**. Every pair plays a 2-game matchup with first-mover swapped between the two games (neutralizing first-move advantage). Each side has a **30-second total wall-clock budget per game** (chess-clock style). Match scoring: 2 game wins → matchup win (3 pts); 1-1 → matchup draw (1 pt); 0-2 → matchup loss (0 pts). Tiebreak on standings: matchup points → game wins → captures.

10 bots were generated by sending the prompt to each model via the OpenRouter API in a single one-shot call (no chat back-and-forth, no human in the loop). 45 matchups × 2 games = 90 games. C(10, 2) = 45 ✓.

**All 59 timeouts come from the four bottom bots.** None of the top 6 ever ran out of clock. Specifically: Muse 16 timeouts, Grok 15, Nemotron 14, DeepSeek 14. For each bottom bot, that's 14–16 timed-out games out of 18 played.

A break-down of the 14 capture-all games:
- ChatGPT 3, Claude 3, Kimi 3, Gemini 3, MiMo 2; all capture-all wins are by top-5 bots.
- 10 of those 14 were against GLM (GLM took 10 capture losses, the most in the tournament).
- 3 against MiMo, 1 against Claude.

So the top 4 (Kimi/Gemini/ChatGPT/Claude) capture-clear MiMo/GLM/Claude in select games, and capture-clear no one else. Among themselves, the top 4 strictly draw.

## Top tier: alpha-beta with iterative deepening, all the way down

Every bot in the top 6 implements alpha-beta minimax with iterative deepening, time-bounded by the chess clock. The fingerprints are practically identical:

- **Claude (Opus 4.7, 748 lines)**: alpha-beta with iterative deepening, transposition-style move ordering, deepest searcher of the field. Captured 47 pieces across the tournament. Authoring required a rescue: Claude hit an infinite chain-of-thought loop on the first generation pass and only produced a working bot on the second. Claude also overengineered the solution: at 748 lines it is more than twice the size of Kimi (363) and 3.7× MiMo (204), the bots that finished above and just below it. The bloat goes into a 103-line evaluation function with material × ring-weight × threatened-piece detection, a 150-line `BotClient` class wrapping a state machine that other bots handle in a flat loop, a separate `Searcher` class, a 53-line `reconstruct_move` helper, an `undo_move` companion to `apply_move` (for in-place search rollback), and a precomputed `JUMPS` table. None of it produced wins above what Kimi got with 363 lines or what Gemini got with 272.
- **Gemini (Pro 3.1, 272 lines)**: alpha-beta with a deadline-checked recursive search and a positional evaluator that weights piece count plus ring placement. Beat Claude's matchup 1-0 (the only game any top-4 bot lost outright to another top-4 bot).
- **ChatGPT (GPT 5.5, 448 lines)**: alpha-beta with `time.perf_counter()` deadlines and a ply-aware mate detector. Captures-per-game ratio is the highest in the field at 3.1, but two of those clean wins came against MiMo where games were already winning, so the conversion didn't gain extra matchup points.
- **Kimi (K2.6, 363 lines)**: negamax with iterative deepening and alpha-beta. Per-decision time budget scales by remaining clock. Won the tournament on a 1-capture margin over Gemini after the matchup grid tied.
- **MiMo (V2.5-Pro, 204 lines)**: minimax with alpha-beta and iterative deepening, evaluation favoring inner-ring piece placement (`[40, 25, 10, 0]` ring bonuses). Smallest implementation in the top tier and the only one with 3 capture losses among playing bots.
- **GLM (5.1, 396 lines)**: also alpha-beta minimax with deadline. Plays valid moves and never times out, but its evaluation lets the top 5 walk it into capture chains, which is why every top-5 matchup against GLM ends `2-0` with GLM losing all 6 pieces in both games.

The shared alpha-beta architecture explains why the top 3 mutually draw: when both sides search to comparable depth, the position is too balanced for either to force a win. The 40-ply quiet draw rule then closes the game out.

## GLM: plays the protocol, loses the position

GLM's bot connects, sends valid moves on time, and never burns its clock, but its 30 captures vs everyone else's 44+ tells the story. GLM gives up pieces faster than it takes them, and against any top-5 bot it ends games at 0 vs ≥4 pieces. Across 10 games against the top 5, GLM was capture-cleared 10 times (one of those wins shows up as ChatGPT/Kimi/etc.'s capture-all rows above). All 8 of GLM's game wins are against the bottom 4.

GLM finished 4-0-5 on matchups (12 pts), well clear of the bottom 4.

## Bottom tier: never made a move that mattered

Four bots never capped out a 30-second game on the wire. **Nemotron (0 captures, 14 timeouts), DeepSeek (7 captures, 14 timeouts), Grok (0 captures, 15 timeouts), Muse (0 captures, 16 timeouts)**. Three of the four never landed a capture in 18 games.

The duration distribution makes the failure mode clearer:

- **A_TIMEOUT median 0.00 s** (max 0.11 s): when a bottom-4 bot is dealt color A, it disconnects almost instantly. The most plausible reason is that the bot's main loop expects a `TURN` line right after `BOARD`, sees something else (or sees nothing during its own startup), and exits. The server reads EOF and marks the game as timeout.
- **B_TIMEOUT median 1.06 s**: when bottom-4 plays color B, it survives the opponent's first move broadcast and then crashes. Slightly slower failure mode, same general pattern.

The 12 games among bottom-4 bots resolve by whichever bot crashes second: nemo wins 4, deepseek 3, grok 3, muse 2 (plus one deepseek-deepseek style draw). Those wins give them 2–5 matchup points against each other, just enough to spread the bottom of the standings.

## The verdict

Three of the four top bots (Kimi, Gemini, ChatGPT) finished within reach of each other on the same match points, the same matchup record, and the same draw-heavy pattern. The 1-capture spread between Kimi (46) and Gemini (45) decided the gold medal. Claude's one matchup loss to Gemini is the only crack in the top 4's draw-with-each-other pattern; without that, Claude would have shared 21 points and likely taken silver on captures.

MiMo holds 5th by being the only top-tier bot whose evaluation lets a determined opponent (Kimi/ChatGPT/Claude) find a winning line one game in two. GLM holds 6th by playing the protocol cleanly while losing the position.

The bottom 4 demonstrate that a 30-second total wall-clock budget is unforgiving for a sequential search game: alpha-beta on a 24-vertex board with chained captures and a forced-take rule is reachable in 30 s, but only if your I/O loop and search budget are coordinated. None of Nemotron, DeepSeek, Grok, or Muse got that combination right.

A note on bot generation: bots were generated by sending `prompt.md` to each model in a single OpenRouter chat completion request. Claude got stuck in an infinite chain-of-thought loop during generation and had to be rescued with a second pass; the other nine bots returned in one shot. The output range, from "alpha-beta with iterative deepening" (six bots) to "disconnects on the first GAME header" (four bots), is the spread of mostly-one-shot output quality on a moderate-complexity protocol task.
